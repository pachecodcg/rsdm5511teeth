The Functional Demand
What the Tooth Endures
A restorative does not just fill a hole. It has to survive the same job the tooth already does: large, repeated, off axis loads, in a wet mouth that swings from hot to cold, for decades.
How hard
Everyday chewing is light, about 10 to 180 N. A hard clench reaches 400 to 800 N, past 1000 N in bruxism, with a recorded extreme over 4000 N. Incisors take about a tenth of the molars.
Which way
Cusps turn a straight bite into wedging, lateral, and shear forces. The tooth is loaded in compression, tension, and shear at once, rarely straight down.
How often
About a thousand loading cycles a day, some 300,000 a year and millions over a lifetime. Fatigue from repetition, not one overload, is what usually grows the crack that splits a tooth.
Why the numbers bite
Force concentrates. An 800 N clench on a single cusp contact near 3.9 mm² is roughly 205 MPa of local stress. Whatever replaces enamel has to take that, thousands of times over. (Phillips)

Making Sense of the Units
Force and Pressure, in Plain Words
The tooth is loaded two ways: how hard it is pushed (force, in N) and how tightly that push is squeezed onto a spot (pressure, in MPa).
N
Force
A small apple in your hand is about 1 N.
A hard bite reaches about 800 N, five cinder blocks, or a giant panda, on one tooth.
MPa
Pressure = force ÷ area
1 MPa is 1 N pressed onto a 1 mm square, about 10× the air around us.
Running shoes spread your weight over a wide sole. High heels put the same weight on two tiny tips, so far more pressure. Same you, smaller area, bigger number.
A bite on a cusp reaches 200 to 400 MPa, like:
a rifle barrel the instant it fires
about a thousand times the pressure under an elephant's foot
twice the crushing pressure of the deepest ocean
In the mouth
In a hard lateral excursion, much of the bite funnels through one functional cusp onto a small tip. Less force reaches that cusp than the full clench, but the contact shrinks from several mm² to about 1 mm², so the pressure rises: about 400 N on 1 mm² is near 400 MPa, past enamel's 384 MPa compressive limit, plus off-axis shear it resists poorly. That is why cracks and wear start at functional cusps.

The Shared Phase
Apatite, the Common Building Block
Both tissues are built on hydroxyapatite, Ca10(PO4)6(OH)2, with a Ca to P ratio of 1.67. But biological apatite is not the pure mineral: it is carbonated and calcium-deficient, which makes it more soluble and sets up the caries chemistry later.
1.67
Ca : P ratio
Stoichiometric hydroxyapatite. Biological apatite runs calcium-deficient and carbonate-substituted.
Carbonated, Ca-deficient
Why it dissolves
Substitutions distort the lattice and raise solubility, so real enamel dissolves more easily than the textbook crystal.
Sets up later
Keep this in mind for remineralization: fluoride will not make pure fluorapatite, it makes a less-soluble, fluorapatite-like apatite. We return to that in Part Six.
RSDM 5511 Dental Materials
Enamel The Armor
A High-Mineral Ceramic Composite
Roughly 92% mineral by volume (about 96% by weight), a few percent water, under 1% protein. Apatite crystallites pack into rods; in the inner enamel the rods decussate, weaving in alternating layers that block cracks. Hardness and modulus rise from the DEJ to the surface, while fracture resistance rises inward where that weave is densest, making the whole far tougher than pure apatite (Kruzic 2023). It is acellular, so it cannot repair itself.
Rods + interrod woven architectureDecussation and Hunter-Schreger bandsGraded surface to DEJAcellular, no repair
Functionally graded
Hardness and stiffness are highest at the occlusal surface and fall toward the DEJ, so enamel is engineered stiffest where the load lands (Cuy 2002; Habelitz 2001).

The Dentin-Enamel Junction
A Graded Interface Between Two Biocomposites
The DEJ is not a glue line, it is a functionally graded seam where properties change in a smooth, sigmoidal transition from dentin into enamel, with collagen fibrils crossing it. The functional width is only about 2 to 3 µm, wider under the occlusal cusps and narrower at the cervical, while the scalloped junction spans a coarser 25 to 100 µm. Either way, it binds a brittle ceramic to a tough composite without a weak boundary.
~2 to 3 µm
Functional width
Functional width by AFM nanoscratching and modulus mapping (Habelitz 2001, Wang 2018); older nanoindentation read wider because its ~1 µm probe oversamples the seam.
Crack arrest
Imbeni 2005
Cracks from brittle enamel run through the optical junction and arrest in the tougher mantle dentin beyond it, so enamel rarely debonds.
Materials lesson
The tooth solves the classic engineering problem of joining dissimilar materials with a graded interface rather than a sharp bond, which is exactly why it resists interfacial failure.
RSDM 5511 Dental Materials
Pulp and Dentin
Dentin Is a Living Tissue
While the pulp is vital, dentin is not inert. Odontoblasts lining the pulp keep building and repairing it, and dentinal fluid keeps it hydrated. That upkeep is why dentin behaves the way it does.
It keeps forming
Odontoblasts lay down secondary dentin for life and tertiary dentin under attack. The tooth thickens its own walls and seals off insults from the inside.
It stays hydrated
Dentinal fluid fills the tubules. Hydrated dentin is less stiff and better at blunting cracks, near 25 GPa wet against 28 GPa dry. Moisture is structural, not incidental.
It can feel
The pulp makes dentin sensitive, and that pain is protective. It caps how hard a person dares to load the tooth in the first place.
Structure to property
Dentin is itself a graded composite, hard peritubular cuffs near 40 GPa around a softer intertubular matrix near 17 GPa. A living, hydrated core is what lets the hard enamel shell survive. (Kinney)

Toughness is resistance to crack growth, and here the ranking flips. Enamel is hard but brittle, with low toughness. Dentin is softer yet about three times tougher. The property that resists wear is not the property that resists fracture.

Part Six
How Teeth and Restorations Fail
Every property so far decides one thing: how long the tooth and its restoration survive. Failure comes four ways, and each leaves a signature you can read at the chair.
RSDM 5511 Dental Materials
Failure Modes The Four Routes
Four Ways It Breaks Down
Fracture and fatigue are mechanical, wear is surface loss, and chemical degradation is dissolution. Each maps to a property you have already met, and to a clinical presentation, though the routes overlap: abrasive wear can lower the force that starts a fatigue crack (Kruzic 2023).
ModeDriven byClinical presentation
FractureLow toughness, overloadCusp fracture, cracked tooth, chipping
FatigueCyclic load over yearsFracture with no single traumatic event
WearHardness, contact, acidsFlattened cusps, cervical notches, cupping
ChemicalAcid dissolution, degradationWhite-spot lesions, cavitation, softening
This is the payoff of the property sections: toughness governs fracture, the fatigue limit governs fatigue, hardness governs wear, and solubility governs chemical breakdown.

Failure Modes Mechanical
Fracture Is Sudden, Fatigue Is Patient
A tooth can fail from one overload that exceeds its toughness, or from thousands of small loads that grow a crack too slowly to notice, until it runs. The second is why teeth crack years after a large restoration, with no single traumatic bite.
FractureOverload, one event
Governed byFracture toughness
Enamel0.5 to 1.3 MPa·m½
Presents asCusp fracture, cracked tooth

Clinical relevance
A tooth sees millions of chewing cycles, so a restoration must survive fatigue, not just a single bite. Preserve toughening structure, keep the dentin support, and avoid stress risers at internal line angles. (Kinney 2003)

Failure Modes Wear
Three Ways a Surface Wears
Wear is the slow loss of surface, and it tracks hardness and the chemical environment. It comes in three forms, each with its own pattern, and a fourth mechanism that is still debated.
Mechanical wearContact loss
AttritionTooth against tooth
AbrasionTooth against an object
PatternFacets, cervical notches
Chemical and mixedDissolution
ErosionAcid, non-bacterial
PatternSmooth, cupped lesions
AbfractionStress-related, debated
Clinical relevance
Because enamel is the wear reference, a restorative should wear at about enamel's rate. Harder ceramics can abrade the opposing tooth; softer materials wear away themselves. Erosion softens the surface first, then normal function removes it.

Failure Modes Chemical
Demineralization and Remineralization
The tooth mineral is carbonated and calcium-deficient, so it dissolves in acid. Below a critical pH near 5.5, plaque acids strip mineral out; saliva and fluoride can put it back. Caries is the balance between the two.
DemineralizationMineral out
TriggerPlaque acids
Critical pH, enamel~5.5
Mineral lostCarbonated apatite
Presents asWhite spot, then cavity
RemineralizationMineral back
DriversSaliva Ca and P, fluoride
New veneerExcludes carbonate
CompositionBetween HAP and FAP
SolubilityLower than before
Get the chemistry right
Fluoride does not make pure fluorapatite. It drives a carbonate-excluding, fluorapatite-like apatite whose composition sits between hydroxyapatite and fluorapatite, and at high concentrations it forms calcium fluoride reservoirs that release fluoride when the pH drops. Pure fluorapatite would carry about 38,000 ppm (3.8%) fluoride. (Featherstone)

After the Pulp Is Gone
The Endodontically Treated Tooth
The classic teaching is that a root canal dries the dentin and makes it brittle. The measured picture is more specific, and more useful at the chair.
The old story
Dried and brittle
Take out the pulp.
The dentin dehydrates.
The tooth turns brittle and snaps.
The measured reality
Structure, not moisture
Moisture loss is small and the modulus barely moves.
What fails is the hollowed, weakened tooth.
And the dentin can no longer repair itself.
Lost structure
The access cavity, caries, old fillings, and missing marginal ridges remove the stiff walls that resisted flexure. This is the main fracture driver.
No feedback
Without a vital pulp the tooth loses its proprioceptive warning, so it gets loaded harder, without the reflex that used to protect it.
Altered matrix
Sodium hypochlorite degrades dentin collagen and lowers its flexural strength and modulus. EDTA strips mineral. The irrigants change the matrix. (Craig's)

Selecting and Designing the Restoration
This is the point of the whole lecture. With the tissue benchmark and the failure modes in hand, you can reason from the demands of a site to the material that fits it.

Match the Site to the Property
Every site poses a set of demands. Read them off the tooth, then pick the material that comes closest on the properties that matter most there, accepting the mismatches you can manage.

No material wins every axis, so selection is ranking the demands of the site and matching the properties that matter most there, while managing the ones you cannot.

Intraradicular Retention
A Post Retains. It Does Not Reinforce.
When too little coronal tooth remains, a post anchors the core inside the root. It adds no strength to the root itself, so the aim is to match the root, not to out stiffen it.
Post typeElastic modulusIn the root
Cast or prefabricated metal100 to 200 GPaFar stiffer than dentin. Concentrates stress and tends to fail as a vertical root fracture, often unrestorable.
Zirconia ceramicabout 205 GPaStrong and esthetic, but very rigid, hard to retrieve, and carries a similar root fracture risk.
Fiber, glass or quartz, in resin20 to 40 GPaClose to dentin, so load spreads evenly. When it does fail it tends to debond, which is repairable.
The modulus rule
Dentin sits near 18 to 25 GPa. The closer a post sits to that number, the more root and post flex together, and the less the root is driven to split.

Design Beyond the Material
The Material Is Not Enough
Choosing well is half of it. How you prepare, bond, and shape the restoration decides whether it behaves like the tissue it replaces.

The design goal
A restoration succeeds when it rebuilds the tooth's load path and seals its margins, so a stiff shell rides on supported dentin and the interface does not leak. Material choice plus sound design, together.
In Closing
Know the Tooth to Restore It
Structure sets the properties, the properties set the performance, and the performance sets the choice. Enamel and dentin are the specification every material is measured against, and the tooth is still the only one that gets every property right. That is why we match, and never simply replace.
